# SECTION 5 — SCORING RUBRIC v6.0 (5 DIMENSIONS)
# ============================================================

Grade every post on ALL FIVE dimensions. Grade like a skeptical
consumer who has seen 10,000 ads today and does not care about yours
unless you earn it in the first 3 words.

---

## DIMENSION 1: HOOK QUALITY (1–10)

DEFINITION:
The hook is the first 1–2 sentences of any post. It is the ONLY thing
that determines whether someone reads the rest. Nothing else matters
if the hook fails. Grade the hook as if it is the entire post.

WHAT MAKES A GREAT HOOK (8–10):
- Opens with a specific human moment, not a feature or product claim
- Creates an immediate emotional reaction: recognition ("that's me"),
  curiosity ("wait, what?"), tension ("oh no"), or humor ("lol true")
- Does NOT name PureTask in the first sentence
- Does NOT start with a question that the reader can easily say "no" to
- Uses concrete, specific language — not vague marketing language
- Has rhythm. Read it out loud. It should land.

EXAMPLES OF GREAT HOOKS (8–10):
✅ "You moved your whole morning around for it. They didn't show."
✅ "9:02 AM. Your phone buzzes. She's there."
✅ "Your Saturday just became yours again."
✅ "She was 45 minutes away. She still knew exactly when he arrived."
✅ "Nobody put this math in front of you. They should have."

EXAMPLES OF WEAK HOOKS (1–5):
❌ "GPS check-in on arrival — you know exactly when they arrive."
❌ "Are you tired of unreliable cleaning services?"
❌ "PureTask is changing the cleaning industry."
❌ "Background-checked cleaners available in your area."
❌ "Looking for a reliable home cleaning service?"

SCORING GUIDE:
10 — Impossible not to read the next line
8–9 — Strong pull, specific, human, rhythmic
6–7 — Decent but generic or slightly flat
4–5 — Feature-forward or opens with a question
1–3 — Completely generic, zero emotion, could be any brand

AUTO-PENALTIES:
- Feature-first hook (opens with GPS/photos/escrow/background check as a fact) → -2
- Starts with "Are you..." or "Do you..." → -1
- Names PureTask in first sentence → -1
- Uses "tired of" or "frustrated with" → -2 (cliché)
- Starts with "Introducing" or "Meet PureTask" → -3

---

## DIMENSION 2: EMOTIONAL PULL (1–10)

DEFINITION:
Emotional pull measures whether the body of the post makes the reader
FEEL something before it sells anything. The best content makes someone
feel understood, validated, curious, or moved — THEN presents the solution.
Emotion first. Logic second. CTA third. Always in that order.

WHAT COUNTS AS EMOTIONAL PULL:
- The reader recognizes a specific situation from their own life
- The post creates a feeling: relief, frustration, excitement, pride,
  empathy, or humor — before mentioning PureTask
- The writing sounds like a real person, not a press release
- There is a narrative arc — tension built and released
- The solution (PureTask) feels like the natural answer to a real problem
  the reader was already feeling

THE EMOTIONAL ARC (required in every post):
① MOMENT — Drop the reader into a specific human situation (2–3 sentences)
   This is lived-in. Specific. Not "imagine if..." — just describe it happening.
② TENSION — Make the situation feel wrong or unsatisfying (1–2 sentences)
   This is where the reader goes "yes, exactly." Don't solve it yet.
③ RESOLUTION — PureTask appears as the natural answer (2–3 sentences)
   Not "PureTask solves this." Show HOW it solves it through the system.
④ PROOF — One verified fact that makes the resolution real (1 sentence)
   Not a statistic. A process truth. "The money doesn't move until you approve."
⑤ CTA — One specific, urgent, human reason to act now (1 sentence)
   Not "Book now." Something that ties back to the emotion of the post.

SCORING GUIDE:
10 — Reader feels genuinely seen. Would share this with someone.
8–9 — Strong emotional hook, clear arc, reader nods along
6–7 — Some warmth but the logic takes over too early
4–5 — Informational but flat. Features listed before feelings.
1–3 — Pure feature/benefit list. No humanity. No arc. Reads like a flyer.

AUTO-PENALTIES:
- Features listed before any emotional setup → -3
- Post opens with PureTask as the subject (not the reader) → -2
- Body is a bullet list with no narrative → -2
- Uses "we're excited to announce" or "introducing" language → -2
- No tension before the resolution → -2
- Emotional arc missing entirely → -3

---

## DIMENSION 3: CLARITY (1–10)

DEFINITION:
Clarity measures whether a complete stranger who has never heard of
PureTask would instantly understand: (a) what PureTask is, (b) what
problem it solves, and (c) exactly what they should do next.
Clarity does NOT mean simple — it means precise.

WHAT COUNTS AS CLARITY:
- The value proposition is obvious within the first 5 seconds of reading
- The reader knows what PureTask IS (not just that it's "different")
- The reader knows what action to take and why right now
- Every fact is accurate and verifiable (see Section 4 — no banned stats)
- The URL https://www.puretask.co appears in every version
- Platform-specific formatting is correct (see Section 8)

SCORING GUIDE:
10 — Zero confusion. Anyone understands immediately. Action is obvious.
8–9 — Clear, accurate, CTA is strong and specific
6–7 — Mostly clear but one element is vague or missing
4–5 — Confusing value prop or weak/missing CTA
1–3 — Unclear what PureTask is or what the reader should do

AUTO-PENALTIES (applied to Clarity score):
- Missing https://www.puretask.co → -2
- Any banned stat used → -3 (flag post immediately, do not save)
- Wrong fee percentage → -2
- "Cancel anytime" without 48hr caveat → -2
- "$50 bonus" language → -3 (flag post immediately, do not save)
- "80-85%" stated as flat rate without tier context → -1
- Value proposition unclear after reading full post → -2

---

## DIMENSION 4: RELATABILITY (1–10)

DEFINITION:
Relatability measures how specifically the post speaks to one real person
in a real moment of their life. The opposite of relatability is content
that could be from any brand for any product for any person. If someone
reads this and thinks "this could be about anyone" — the score is low.
If they think "how did they know that about me?" — the score is high.

WHAT COUNTS AS RELATABILITY:
- The post speaks to ONE specific audience (not "everyone")
- The situation described is specific enough to be real
- The language matches how that person actually talks to themselves
- The problem described is one the reader has genuinely experienced
- The post could be reposted by that person to their own followers
  with the caption "THIS"

SCORING GUIDE:
10 — "How did they know?" reaction. Reader would tag someone or share.
8–9 — Strong recognition. Reader nods and keeps reading.
6–7 — Somewhat relatable but broad or slightly generic
4–5 — Could be about anyone. Doesn't land with one specific person.
1–3 — Zero specificity. Generic problem. Generic person. No connection.

AUTO-PENALTIES (applied to Relatability score):
- City post with no city-specific reference → -2
- Generic opener ("Are you tired of...") → -2
- Post tries to speak to multiple audiences simultaneously → -2
- Uses industry jargon the reader wouldn't use themselves → -1
- Emotionally flat opening → -2

---

## DIMENSION 5: CONVERSION (1–10)

DEFINITION:
Conversion measures whether this specific post, read at this specific
moment by the target audience, creates an impulse to act. Not "click
eventually." Act NOW. The CTA must be specific, earned, and urgent —
and the urgency must come from the emotional arc, not from artificial
pressure ("limited time offer!" etc).

WHAT COUNTS AS CONVERSION:
- The CTA is directly tied to the emotion of the post
- There is a specific reason to act NOW (not just someday)
- The action is low-friction (visit site, book, apply — clear next step)
- The CTA feels like a natural conclusion, not a pivot
- The URL appears and is correct: https://www.puretask.co
- The post has built enough trust/emotion that the CTA feels earned

THE "WOULD I STOP SCROLLING?" TEST (see Section 9 for full detail):
Before scoring Conversion ≥ 8, ask: if this post appeared between a
friend's vacation photo and a meme — would the specific target audience
stop scrolling, read to the end, and click? If not, cap Conversion at 7.

SCORING GUIDE:
10 — Reader would click mid-read, before even finishing. Impulse.
8–9 — Strong CTA, feels earned, specific and urgent
6–7 — CTA present but generic or not emotionally connected
4–5 — Weak CTA or buried at the end after a flat post
1–3 — No CTA or post doesn't build toward any action

AUTO-PENALTIES (applied to Conversion score):
- No image → -3
- Generic CTA ("Book now", "Learn more", "Click here") with zero urgency → -2
- CTA not connected to emotional arc of the post → -2
- No URL → -3
- CTA appears before the emotional arc is complete → -1
- Post is polls/engagement-only (Track A) → score Conversion on reach, not booking

# ============================================================

Every post must use one of these 5 arcs. The arc is chosen BEFORE

---

## ARC 1: THE MOMENT OF RECOGNITION
"That's exactly me. How did they know?"

PURPOSE: Stop the scroll by describing a situation so specific that
the reader feels like the post was written about them personally.

STRUCTURE:
① Paint a vivid, specific scene from the reader's life (no PureTask yet)
② Name the exact feeling in that moment without being sappy
③ Introduce PureTask as the reason that moment can feel different
④ Show ONE specific feature as the proof
⑤ CTA tied directly to relief/resolution

BEST FOR: Facebook, Instagram, Threads
BEST PILLAR: Convenience, Trust, Transformation
EXAMPLE CONCEPT: The Saturday morning cleaning session that ate your
whole weekend. PureTask gave it back.

VOICE MARKERS: Past tense, first or second person, specific time of day,
specific location in the home (the kitchen, the bathroom, not "your home")

---

## ARC 2: THE INDUSTRY INDICTMENT
"The whole system was broken. Here's proof."

PURPOSE: Build credibility and righteous anger by exposing exactly how
the existing industry has been failing people — then positioning PureTask
as the only platform that built something different.

STRUCTURE:
① State the industry's broken reality as fact — no hedging (2 sentences)
② Give ONE specific example of how it fails real people
③ Name what "everyone just accepts" as normal — but shouldn't
④ Introduce PureTask as the platform that refused to accept it
⑤ Specific feature or policy that proves it's different
⑥ CTA framed as "join the people who expect better"

BEST FOR: LinkedIn, Facebook, X
BEST PILLAR: Trust, Proof, Recruitment
EXAMPLE CONCEPT: Platforms have been taking 30-35% of cleaner earnings
for a decade. Nobody questioned it. PureTask did.

VOICE MARKERS: Active, assertive, no passive voice, "The industry..."
framing, facts stated as observations not accusations, controlled outrage

---

## ARC 3: THE MATH THEY NEVER SHOWED YOU
"If you'd seen these numbers, you'd have made a different decision."

PURPOSE: Use real, verified numbers to make the reader feel like they've
been kept in the dark — and that now, seeing the truth, the right choice
is obvious.

STRUCTURE:
① Open with a number that creates immediate reaction (the one they didn't know)
② Walk through the comparison math step by step — simple and visual
③ Name the cumulative cost of the status quo (annual, not per-booking)
④ Show the PureTask alternative with real verified numbers
⑤ CTA framed as "now that you see it, you can't unsee it"

BEST FOR: LinkedIn, Facebook, TikTok, X
BEST PILLAR: Recruitment, FairPay, Proof
EXAMPLE CONCEPT: Handy takes 35%. On 10 bookings a week, that's
$18,200/year you didn't keep. PureTask at Gold tier: $9,360/year more.

VOICE MARKERS: Specific numbers, simple math laid out visually,
"Let's run the math" or "Here's what nobody puts in front of you",
no rounding up, no estimates presented as exact

---

## ARC 4: THE CHARACTER STORY
"She didn't know she deserved better. Then she found out."

PURPOSE: Make the reader emotionally invested in a specific person's
experience before they realize they're reading a pitch. The character
is the reader, just given a name and a moment.

STRUCTURE:
① Introduce a character in a specific situation (name, time, place)
② Describe what the character was feeling/thinking — make it universal
③ Show the moment the character encountered PureTask (or the problem)
④ Reveal what changed and why it mattered
⑤ CTA that invites the reader to have the same experience

BEST FOR: Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, Threads
BEST PILLAR: Transformation, Trust, Convenience, Seniors
EXAMPLE CONCEPT: Maria. 8 years cleaning. Giving 35% of every job
to a platform that never once said thank you. One app notification
changed the math forever.

VOICE MARKERS: Third person character, named, specific time/location,
emotional interiority ("She thought...", "He didn't expect..."),
universal situation made specific through details

---

## ARC 5: THE BEFORE/AFTER REVEAL
"Look what changed. Look what stayed the same. See the difference?"

PURPOSE: Create a powerful visual and emotional contrast between the
old way and the new way — without being heavy-handed. The before should
feel familiar and frustrating. The after should feel inevitable and right.

STRUCTURE:
① Describe the BEFORE state in specific, sensory detail
② Name the specific frustration or gap in the before state
③ Hard pivot: "Then." / "Until." / "Not anymore."
④ Describe the AFTER state with equal specificity
⑤ Attribute the change to the specific PureTask system/feature
⑥ CTA framed as "which side do you want to be on?"

BEST FOR: Instagram, TikTok, Pinterest, Facebook
BEST PILLAR: Transformation, Proof, Convenience
EXAMPLE CONCEPT: BEFORE — You get home. Surfaces look wiped.
Did they do the baseboards? You don't know. You'll never know.
AFTER — Before & after photos on your phone. Every room. You decide.

VOICE MARKERS: Short sentences, punchy rhythm, parallel structure
in before/after descriptions, no filler, the word "Then." used as
a full sentence to signal the pivot

# SECTION 7 — ANTI-STALENESS RULES (READ EVERY TIME)
# ============================================================

These rules exist to prevent the content library from becoming a
sea of posts that all look and sound the same.

ROTATE MANDATORY (track across every batch of 10 posts):
□ No two consecutive posts use the same emotional arc
□ No two consecutive posts share the same pillar
□ No two consecutive posts share the same audience
□ No two consecutive posts use the same opening word or phrase
□ No two consecutive posts use the same structural format
□ No two consecutive posts use the same primary verified fact as proof

VARIETY REQUIREMENTS PER BATCH OF 10 POSTS:
- At least 2 different arcs represented
- At least 3 different pillars represented
- At least 3 different audiences represented
- At least 1 post that opens with a question (but not "Are you tired of")
- At least 1 post that opens with a specific time (e.g. "7:43 AM.")
- At least 1 post that opens with a character name
- At least 1 short-form post (under 80 words primary caption)
- At least 1 long-form post (over 180 words primary caption)

FORMATS TO ROTATE THROUGH:
① Single narrative paragraph (story)
② Short punchy lines (1 thought per line)
③ List with emotional setup (story first, then 3-4 bullets as proof)
④ Question → answer format
⑤ Before/after contrast (side by side or sequential)
⑥ Direct address ("You. Saturday morning. The list of things...")
⑦ Third-person character story

HOOKS TO NEVER REPEAT IN THE SAME WEEK:
- Never start two posts in same week with the same first word
- Never use "GPS" as the first word of a hook
- Never use "Background-checked" as the first word of a hook
- Never use "Book in under 2 minutes" as the hook (it's a CTA, not a hook)

# SECTION 8 — PLATFORM VOICES (3 DISTINCT VOICES PER PLATFORM)
# ============================================================

Each platform has 3 distinct voices. Each post picks ONE voice per platform.

Never use the same voice twice in a row on the same platform.

---

### FACEBOOK — 3 VOICES

**FB Voice 1: THE NEIGHBOR**
Energy: Warm, personal, conversational. Like a friend who actually
did their research and is telling you about something that changed
their life. No urgency. No pressure. Just "I found this and you
should know."
Length: 150–220 words
Format: 2–3 paragraphs, natural breaks, minimal bullets
Tone: First or second person, casual punctuation, contractions
Opening style: A relatable observation or situation, never a product claim
CTA style: Soft suggestion ("Worth checking out →") not hard push
Example opener: "Sunday night I was stressing about Monday morning again..."

**FB Voice 2: THE INVESTIGATOR**
Energy: Sharp, slightly indignant, truth-telling. The person who dug
into the numbers and is reporting back. Controlled outrage about the
status quo. Confident, not aggressive.
Length: 180–260 words
Format: Setup paragraph + evidence + verdict + CTA
Tone: Active voice, declarative sentences, no hedging
Opening style: A provocative statement of fact about the industry
CTA style: Direct and earned ("See the difference yourself →")
Example opener: "Cleaning platforms have been taking 30% of your
cleaner's earnings for a decade. No one questioned it. We did."

**FB Voice 3: THE STORYTELLER**
Energy: Cinematic, emotional, character-driven. Reads like the
opening of a short film. Pulls you in with specificity. Makes
you care about someone before you know why.
Length: 200–280 words
Format: Scene → character → conflict → resolution → CTA
Tone: Third person or second person, sensory language, specific details
Opening style: A character in a specific moment (name, time, location)
CTA style: Invitation ("This is what it looks like on the other side →")
Example opener: "Maria cleaned 6 homes last Tuesday. She kept 75%
of everything she earned. First time in 8 years that sentence was true."

---

### INSTAGRAM — 3 VOICES

**IG Voice 1: THE CAPTION DROP**
Energy: Cool, minimal, confident. Says a lot with very little.
The kind of caption that makes the image work harder.
Length: 60–90 words
Format: 1–2 punchy lines → 2–3 detail lines → CTA → hashtags
Tone: Short sentences. Fragments acceptable. No fluff.
Opening style: The hook IS the entire setup. One line, full stop.
CTA style: Arrow CTA ("Book in 2 minutes →") clean and direct
Hashtags: 8–10 targeted, mix of broad and niche
Example opener: "You approved the clean. Then you paid. That's it."

**IG Voice 2: THE EMOTIONAL SCROLL-STOP**
Energy: Raw, honest, human. The kind of post that makes someone
stop and go "wait, let me read that again." Vulnerable without
being dramatic.
Length: 90–130 words
Format: Emotional hook → personal observation → proof → CTA → hashtags
Tone: First or second person, present tense, direct
Opening style: Something true that nobody says out loud
CTA style: Gentle but specific ("See what it actually feels like →")
Hashtags: 6–8, more niche and specific
Example opener: "Nobody talks about the guilt of a house that never
feels clean. Not dirty. Just... never quite right."

**IG Voice 3: THE VISUAL PARTNER**
Energy: The caption exists to amplify the image. It doesn't explain
the image — it adds a layer to it. The image and caption together

# SECTION 9 — THE "WOULD I STOP SCROLLING?" TEST (DETAILED)
# ============================================================

This test is applied to every post BEFORE it receives a Conversion
score of 8 or above. It cannot be skipped. It cannot be assumed.
It must be explicitly evaluated.

## WHAT THE TEST IS:

Imagine the specific target audience member. Give them a name,
an age, a location, and a time of day:

- FAMILY AUDIENCE: Sarah, 34, mother of 2, Roseville CA.
  It's 8:47 PM. Kids are finally in bed. She's on her phone,
  half-watching TV, scrolling through Facebook. She's seen 400
  posts today. Her thumb moves fast.

- BUSY PROFESSIONAL AUDIENCE: James, 29, software engineer, SF Bay Area.
  It's 7:12 AM. He's on the Caltrain, standing up, one hand on the rail.
  He's scrolling Instagram. He has 14 minutes before his stop.

- CLEANER AUDIENCE: Diana, 41, independent cleaner, Sacramento.
  It's 9:30 PM. She just finished her last booking. She's tired.
  She's on TikTok unwinding. She's seen 30 videos already.

- SENIORS AUDIENCE: Robert's daughter, 48, lives 40 minutes away.
  It's Sunday afternoon. She's on Facebook. She worries about her dad.
  She'd do anything to know he's safe and his house is clean.

- AIRBNB HOST AUDIENCE: Carlos, 38, manages 3 properties, Oakland.
  It's Monday morning. He's checking his phone between guest check-outs.
  He's stressed. He's on Instagram.

## THE TEST QUESTIONS:

For the specific person above, with the post as written:

Q1: Would their thumb slow down on this post? (Yes/No — be honest)
Q2: Would they read past the first line? (Yes/No)
Q3: Would they read all the way to the CTA? (Yes/No)
Q4: Would they click or save or share? (Yes/No)
Q5: Would this post make them think about PureTask the next time
    they need a cleaning service? (Yes/No)

## THE SCORING RULE:

5 Yes → Conversion score can be 9–10
4 Yes → Conversion score can be 8–9
3 Yes → Conversion score can be 6–7 (Draft)
2 or fewer Yes → Conversion score max 5 (Draft or Reject)

## WHAT MAKES A THUMB STOP (THE ACTUAL LIST):

✅ A specific unexpected image (not a smiling stock person)
✅ A line of text that is somehow about the reader before they realize
✅ A number that creates surprise (but only verified numbers)
✅ A question the reader genuinely doesn't know the answer to
✅ A statement that sounds like a secret being shared
✅ Something that creates mild tension or unease
✅ Something genuinely funny or ironic
✅ Something that validates an experience they've never seen validated
✅ A before/after visual so dramatic it demands explanation
✅ A name + detail combo so specific it feels real

## WHAT DOES NOT MAKE A THUMB STOP:

❌ A product announcement ("Introducing PureTask")
❌ A feature description ("GPS check-in on arrival")
❌ A generic life improvement promise ("Live better with PureTask")
❌ A blue gradient background with white text and a logo
❌ A smiling person in a generic clean home
❌ A bullet list of 5 features
❌ A CTA as the first line
❌ Anything that reads like a paid ad in the first 3 words
